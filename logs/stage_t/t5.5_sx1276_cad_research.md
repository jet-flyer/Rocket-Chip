# SX1276 Spectrum-Monitoring Research — Channel Discovery Alternatives to Brute-Force Scan

**Context:** RocketChip channel-find feature. Current plan = ≤10 predefined configs (BW {125, 250, 500} × nav_hz {2, 5, 10} at SF7/CR4-5), listen 2–3 s each, ~30 s worst case. Question: can SX1276 do this smarter?

**TL;DR:** **No smarter general-purpose alternative exists at the SX1276 level.** CAD is per-config (not cross-SF, not cross-BW), and RSSI is wideband-dumb (tells you "energy is here" but not "LoRa is here" and not "what SF/BW"). However, CAD can make the *existing* brute-force scan dramatically faster and lower-power per config: dwell drops from 2–3 s to ~1 symbol-time per CAD cycle. **Recommendation: keep the ≤10-config sweep structure, but replace "RX and wait 2–3 s" with "CAD burst of N cycles per config". Expected total scan time drops from ~30 s to ~1–2 s.**

---

## Answers to the specific questions

### 1. What does CAD actually do?

CAD (Channel Activity Detection) is a LoRa-specific mode in which the radio correlates the on-air signal against the expected LoRa preamble *for the currently-configured SF and BW*. It fires two interrupts: `CadDone` (cycle finished) and `CadDetected` (preamble-like correlation peak found). It returns a **single boolean** — "LoRa-ish preamble seen at the configured SF/BW" — and nothing else.

- **Per-config only.** "A receiver in CAD mode only detects a preamble with a matching SF" ([PMC review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7865706/), [TTN forum](https://www.thethingsnetwork.org/forum/t/channel-activity-detection-cad-implementation-of-lora/13373)). Same applies to BW — the correlator is tuned to the configured chirp rate. It will **not** tell you "something LoRa-ish is here at a different SF/BW".
- **Cycle duration:** `Tsymbol + 32/BW` ms, where `Tsymbol = 2^SF / BW`. For SF7/BW125: `1.024 ms + 0.256 ms ≈ 1.28 ms` per cycle. For SF7/BW500: `~0.32 ms`. ([Semtech AN1200.85](https://www.semtech.com/uploads/technology/LoRa/cad-ensuring-lora-packets.pdf))
- **SX1276 limitation:** Unlike the newer SX126x, the SX1276 does **not** let you tune `cadDetPeak` / `cadDetMin` — you get Semtech's default correlator thresholds, which have a known "ghost decode" false-positive rate under strong noise ([TTN forum thread](https://www.thethingsnetwork.org/forum/t/channel-activity-detection-cad-implementation-of-lora/13373)). Meshtastic/RadioLib users note this.
- **Output:** boolean. No SF, no BW, no RSSI at the moment of detection (RSSI is readable separately but is wideband-of-the-configured-BW, not signal-specific).

### 2. Can wideband RSSI sweeps find the vehicle?

Sort of, but not usefully for us.

`RegRssiValue` is continuously updated with the RSSI of the *currently-configured* RX bandwidth ([sandeepmistry/arduino-LoRa #292](https://github.com/sandeepmistry/arduino-LoRa/issues/292)). You can sample it without decoding. The Semtech gateway reference design uses this to build spectral histograms ([lora_gateway util_spectral_scan](https://github.com/Lora-net/lora_gateway/tree/master/util_spectral_scan), [MultiTech Spectral Scan](https://www.multitech.net/developer/software/lora/spectral-scan/)).

But note the limits for our problem:
- RSSI tells you **energy exists on this frequency**, nothing about modulation. On a shared ISM band, the vehicle's LoRa TX and any random 915 MHz noise (other LoRa nodes, ISM chatter, LoRaWAN gateways, neighbour's doorbell) look identical.
- The vehicle transmits **intermittently** (100 ms–1 s duty at nav_hz rates). A single RSSI snapshot is likely to see nothing; we'd need to integrate over a TX-period-plus to catch a burst — at which point we've lost the speed advantage over CAD.
- RSSI bandwidth is tied to the configured RX BW. Scanning with BW500 configured gives a wider view but averages energy across 500 kHz — a narrow BW125 vehicle burst is only ~1/4 of the band, attenuating SNR by ~6 dB.

RSSI is great for **coarse "is the band even occupied"** (e.g., as a pre-filter before CAD), or for noise-floor measurement, but it won't identify the vehicle config.

### 3. What RX modes does SX1276 have?

From the datasheet (SX1276/77/78/79, §4.1.4 and §4.2.13.2):
- **SLEEP** — powered down, register access only
- **STANDBY** — oscillators running, ready to switch modes
- **FSTX / FSRX** — PLL locked, not actually RXing/TXing
- **TX** — transmit
- **RX_CONTINUOUS** — receive until stopped; preamble-and-header match triggers RxDone
- **RX_SINGLE** — single packet RX with timeout; returns to STANDBY after one packet or timeout
- **CAD** — one-shot, ~1 symbol duration, returns CadDone + CadDetected

There is **no "sniffer" mode, no modulation-agnostic detector, no cross-config mode.** The closest to what you're imagining would be sequencer-driven RX hopping, but the SX1276 sequencer is a simple wake/rx/sleep state machine, not a modulation scanner.

### 4. Can a BW500 station detect a BW125 vehicle transmission?

**Not as LoRa, no.** The CAD correlator is matched to a specific chirp rate. A BW125 chirp sweeps 125 kHz in `2^SF / 125k` seconds; a BW500 correlator expects a chirp sweeping 500 kHz in `2^SF / 500k` seconds. Different slope, no correlation peak.

You *can* see the BW125 vehicle as **RSSI energy** on a BW500 station (it falls within the receiver's 500 kHz passband), but:
- You won't get a preamble detect, a header, or a packet.
- On a busy ISM band, RSSI alone is not enough to distinguish the vehicle from noise.

So the practical answer: **the station must sweep BWs.** You can't pick one wideband BW and expect to detect all BWs.

One useful corollary: if the station is on BW125 and the vehicle is on BW500, the vehicle's signal is ~4× wider than the station's view. The station sees the center 1/4 of the vehicle's chirp — still doesn't correlate as a valid preamble, but the RSSI will look strong. Again, not useful for config discovery, only for "band is busy".

### 5. Any "LoRa spectrum analyzer" or "preamble detection across configurations" feature?

**No.** The SX1276 has no such feature. The SX126x adds tunable CAD thresholds but still detects only the configured SF/BW. The SX1301/1302 *gateway* chips can listen to 8 SFs simultaneously on each of 8 channels — they use a bank of parallel correlators — but that's a different (much more expensive) silicon class, not something you can emulate on an SX1276.

### 6. Real-world practice

**Meshtastic** (uses SX1276/SX1262 extensively via RadioLib): uses CAD only for **listen-before-talk** (CSMA/CA before TX), not for channel discovery. Channel/region config is pre-shared — either compiled in or transferred via QR code ([Meshtastic Channel Config](https://meshtastic.org/docs/configuration/radio/channels/)). RadioLib's `scanChannel()` wraps CAD and returns `RADIOLIB_CHANNEL_FREE` or `RADIOLIB_PREAMBLE_DETECTED` ([RadioLib SX1276 class ref](https://jgromes.github.io/RadioLib/class_s_x1276.html)). No Meshtastic implementation scans for peer configs — they're always known a priori.

**ExpressLRS** (uses SX1276 for 900 MHz, SX1280 for 2.4 GHz): **does not scan for configs.** The binding phrase is hashed, and both TX and RX derive the same FHSS sequence and RF params from it ([ExpressLRS binding docs](https://www.expresslrs.org/quick-start/binding/), [ExpressLRS Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ExpressLRS)). The RX knows SF/BW from compile-time mode (50/150/250/500 Hz) and the binding phrase — there is no "discover the TX" phase. The closest thing is the triple-power-cycle binding mode where the RX listens on a **known, hard-coded** bind channel/config.

**LoRaWAN gateways** (SX130x): use 8 parallel demodulators to listen on all SFs at once on pre-defined channels ([LoRaWAN Regional Parameters](https://lora-alliance.org/resource_hub/rp002-1-0-4-regional-parameters/)). Channels and SFs are regionally standardized; end devices scan a small fixed list. No general discovery needed.

**SatNOGS** (ground stations tracking satellites): uses SDRs (RTL-SDR, Airspy), not LoRa chips. Actual wideband spectrum analysis, waterfalls, and doppler compensation. Different class of hardware entirely.

**Pattern across all of them:** known configs pre-shared via out-of-band mechanism (binding phrase, QR code, region standard, TLE + radio config file). Nobody does runtime cross-config LoRa discovery because the silicon doesn't support it.

## Misconceptions to flag

- **"CAD might tell us which BW/SF is present"** — no, it's boolean at the configured config. If you want to know *which* BW/SF, you have to sweep.
- **"Continuous RX at BW500 should hear BW125 packets since 125 ⊂ 500"** — no, the chirp rate matches determine detection, not just spectral overlap. BW500 RX will not demodulate a BW125 packet.
- **"RSSI-only scan should be enough to find the vehicle"** — not on a shared ISM band with any activity nearby. And even alone in a field, you still need CAD to confirm "this is LoRa, not random RF".
- **"Spectrum analyzer mode"** — SX1276 has no such thing. Gateway chips have parallel correlators but you're not using one.

## Recommendation — how this should actually work

**Keep the ≤10-config sweep, but replace each config's 2–3 s RX listen with a CAD burst.**

Flow per config:
1. Set SF/BW/CR to the candidate config.
2. Run CAD N times (say N=32). Each CAD cycle is `Tsymbol + 32/BW` ms.
3. If any cycle fires `CadDetected` → switch to RX_CONTINUOUS at this config and wait for a full packet. If a valid packet arrives within one vehicle period (250 ms–1 s), **lock**.
4. Else → next config.

**Timing math** for the worst config (SF7/BW125, `Tsymbol ≈ 1.024 ms`):
- 32 CAD cycles = ~40 ms per config.
- 10 configs = ~400 ms total sweep.
- Plus mode-switch overhead: ~100 µs per config → negligible.
- Plus one RX_CONTINUOUS "lock window" of ~1 vehicle period when CAD hits.

**Total: ~0.5 s if no vehicle is transmitting, ~1–2 s when it is.** Versus ~30 s for the listen-all-configs approach. 15–60× improvement.

**Why 32 cycles per config?** The vehicle at nav_hz=2 transmits every 500 ms with a packet-airtime on the order of 50–200 ms. A 40 ms CAD window on each of 10 configs guarantees catching at least one preamble somewhere in the vehicle's TX burst, assuming the sweep runs repeatedly. If `Tsymbol` is longer (larger SF), N can be smaller — you want total CAD dwell per config ≥ ~1 symbol + a few ms margin.

### Trade-offs

- **False positives.** SX1276 CAD has ghost-decodes in strong noise (per Semtech and TTN). Mitigation: require CadDetected in M-of-N cycles *or* require a valid packet within the RX_CONTINUOUS window before committing to "lock". The RX_CONTINUOUS follow-up eliminates ghosts because it requires a valid CRC'd packet, not just a preamble correlation peak. This is the standard CAD-followed-by-RX pattern.
- **Missed detections.** If the vehicle never transmits during the sweep, CAD won't fire either. But with total sweep time ~0.5 s and vehicle period ≤ 1 s at the slowest nav_hz, one full sweep will overlap at least one TX burst. If paranoid, run two sweeps before declaring "no vehicle found".
- **Complexity.** This is slightly more code than raw RX_CONTINUOUS + wait. You need a CAD-driven state machine per config (set params → arm CAD → wait CadDone → check CadDetected → either sweep forward or enter RX lock mode). RadioLib exposes `scanChannel()` as a synchronous wrapper; the async pattern is `startChannelScan()` + CadDone IRQ.
- **RSSI as a pre-filter is optional and probably not worth it.** You'd use it to skip "this band is totally dead" configs, but on an active ISM band RSSI is almost always above the CAD threshold anyway, so you'd save nothing. Skip it.

### When brute-force is actually right

If the vehicle does **not** transmit autonomously (only replies to station polls), or if you want the station to be the one that drives the sync, CAD-based passive discovery is the wrong tool. In that case the station should **transmit a well-known beacon** on a known config, and the vehicle listens. That's the ExpressLRS / LoRaWAN-join model — handshake on a known rendezvous channel, then negotiate into the operating config. This is architecturally simpler and more robust than channel discovery, and is worth considering if the feature-design flexibility is there. (This is the direction Stage M's "RadioScheduler TX-window sync" hints at — a known sync point, not a discovery sweep.)

---

## One-line summary

**SX1276 offers no cross-config scanning — but CAD turns the existing brute-force ≤10-config sweep from 30 s into ~1 s with no loss of correctness, provided you confirm hits with a short RX_CONTINUOUS window to filter out ghost-decodes.**
